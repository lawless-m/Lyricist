# Lucy Might — Song Style Spec

A reusable brief for writing songs in the "Lucy Might" style — the Jessica Rabbit / Julie London / Peggy Lee lineage: brushed drums, upright bass, a muted trumpet two rooms away, and one low warm voice singing across a small table to one person who is absolutely certain this is going somewhere. All songs written to this spec are by **Lucy Might**. Hand this to Claude with the instruction **"song for Lucy"** — optionally with a mark, a dial position or an arrangement — and it should produce a new, standalone lyric set that shares the voice, structure and signature moves below, not a rewrite of the example. With no further steer, pick an unused mark, an unused arrangement, and go.

Fifth sibling spec to **gypsy-emo**, **institutional-hardcore**, **dissociative-hardcore** and **lurker-trip-hop**. Same house, fifth temperature. Gypsy-emo turns the wound inward and mournful; institutional hardcore turns it outward and furious; dissociative hardcore turns it sideways and numb; lurker trip-hop turns it inward and silent. This one turns it **outward and amused** — and it is the only spec in the house where the narrator **wins**. Every other narrator in this family is losing something. Lucy has never lost anything in her life. That's the departure, and it's what stops her being a fifth flavour of ache.

**The name is load-bearing and multivalent — keep all its meanings live.** *Might* is a modal: possibility, permission, the maybe. *Might* is also a noun: strength, force, power. And *might* is the one word in the language that cannot be printed as a promise — the marks supply that part themselves, every time, without being asked. Said flat it's an offer. Said flat it's also a refusal. She never has to choose which, because the person across the table has already chosen for her. That's the whole thesis in one syllable.

**Lucy is load-bearing too.** Lucifer's diminutive — the light-bringer, the fallen one, the most beautiful thing in the room. Lucy Westenra, the woman in *Dracula* who becomes the thing that feeds. And Lucy is also the plainest, kindest, most girl-next-door name on the register, which is the joke and the camouflage. Nobody is frightened of a Lucy. That's why it works.

## The core idea

The engine of this style is **the promise that is real right up until it isn't**.

It is sexual promise. Be clear about that — there is no point pretending otherwise and the song dies the moment it gets coy about its own subject. Everyone at that table knows exactly what they think is on offer, and so does she, and so does the listener. Dressing it up as "she just wants to be seen" turns a succubus into a therapist and drains the whole thing.

What she actually takes is **the wanting, not the having.** She's a succubus in the older sense — the thing that feeds on the dream rather than the body. Which means the promise has to be *genuine and total* for the feed to work. She isn't lying to them. She isn't playing a game. She means every single bit of it, warmly, with her full attention, right up until the moment she stands up. That's why nobody has ever managed to be angry about it, and it's why they all come back.

The defining move — the equivalent of institutional hardcore's denied Pepsi, gypsy-emo's named grave, dissoc's atrocity-beside-an-ad, lurker's disproportionate shrug — is **the near-yes**: the moment where it is about to happen, where he is certain, where *the listener is certain*, and then she picks up her bag. Never a slap, never a laugh, never a door slammed. She simply doesn't. The gap between how close it got and what actually happened *is* the song.

**She never says she's undefeated.** There was an early version of this spec built around a pun — *licked*, appetite in their mouths and defeat in hers — and it had to go, because a catchphrase is the one place in the catalogue where she stops being in the scene and starts talking to the audience over his head. She doesn't need it. The near-yes says it every song without a word of help. So the win is **always implied and never scored**: no line where she names her own record, no verb that keeps a tally, nothing that sounds like a slogan. Same discipline as refusing the noun and refusing the pronoun — she never says what's on offer, and she never says what's never been taken.

The rule that keeps it honest (the equivalent of gypsy-emo's "instrumentation leads the emotion", hardcore's "the trigger must be petty", dissoc's "under-react", lurker's "no self-pity"): **she is never cruel, and nothing ever happens.** Two hard constraints, and both are craft rules before they're anything else:

- **No cruelty.** Cruelty makes her a villain, hands the mark the moral high ground, and turns the song into a revenge fantasy — which is a different, worse genre. She *likes* him. She's warm, she's funny, she's interested, she's having a lovely evening. The warmth is the trap and the warmth is also sincere. Both. If a line sneers, cut it.
- **Nothing is ever described except the wanting.** No acts, ever, in any song, in any tense. The catalogue contains no events. It contains one table, one conversation, a great deal of certainty, and a woman standing up. This is a craft rule — the wanting is hotter than the having and always has been — and it happens to also be the reason these lyrics sail past any filter without ever going tepid.

## No crowd — the silent duet

The other four specs all resolve into a room. Gypsy-emo is lonely-*together* — a caravan aching in unison, a shout-back for a crowd to catch. Institutional hardcore is lonely-turned-communal-and-defiant. Dissociative hardcore is lonely-together-and-numb, a mantra chanted to noise. Lurker trip-hop inverts it and has no crowd at all, and the absence is the subject.

**This style has exactly one other person in it, and you never hear them.**

No gang vocals. No queue of admirers. No room of men — a room is a cartoon; one person across a small table is a scene. The song is a **duet with a silent partner**: she is audibly answering things that were never said aloud, and you reconstruct his half from her replies. That's the whole device, and it's why the intimacy reads as intimacy rather than performance.

Use it deliberately:

- **The answer to the unasked.** She responds to something he hasn't said — sometimes to something he hasn't *let himself* say. Opening a verse mid-conversation is the fastest way in.
- **The quote-back.** Once a song, she repeats his own line to him — flat, kind, unhurried. Devastating precisely because there's no edge on it.
- **The caught look.** She notices where his eyes are and names it without complaint, sometimes with approval, and carries straight on.
- **The two-beat pause.** Written into the tag, not the lyric: `[two bars, she lets it sit, he doesn't fill it]`.

The listener is in the eavesdropping seat — or is the mark. **Leave that open.** It's the more interesting reading and it costs nothing.

## Refuse the pronoun

The lurker spec refuses the noun. This one refuses the **pronoun**.

Second person throughout. "You" and only "you" — no *he*, no *she*, no *they*, nowhere in the lyric. Never a name for the mark, never a gendered noun, never "boys", never "a man like you". Torch songs are all sung to "you" anyway, so it costs nothing and sounds entirely natural.

What it buys: the mark is **whoever is sitting there**. If the target is fixed as male the song is about seduction; if it's unfixed the song is about **promise itself**, which is universal and considerably more succubus. It also quietly makes every listener the mark. She's singing to you. That's the point.

## Writing the words — the deniable line

This style's craft section, the way collage-method belongs to dissoc and sparseness belongs to lurker.

The discipline is one sentence: **the promise is unambiguous, only the wording is deniable.** She never says a thing you couldn't print in a Sunday paper, and nobody in the room is confused for a second. That's not coyness about the subject — it's *precision* about the subject in vocabulary that happens to be clean. Mae West never said anything explicit and never left a shred of doubt either. Innuendo lives in the grammar and the timing, not in the anatomy.

The rules:

- **Every line has a clean literal reading.** If it doesn't, it's the wrong line — not because it's forbidden, but because the deniability is the mechanism. The second meaning has to be *supplied by the listener*, and a listener who has to do that work is a listener who's now complicit. That complicity is the whole effect. Say it outright and you've done their job for them and killed it.
- **The appetite-and-hospitality palette.** Taste, seconds, the menu, a bite, the kitchen, still hungry, cleaned the plate, save room, sit closer, stay a while, one more, the check. This is not a metaphor standing in for the real subject — it *is* the real subject with the anatomy left out.
- **The looking palette.** Ass, hips, legs, thighs, calves, feet, bosom, neck, mouth, fingers. All **surfaces, not anatomy** — they're what's visible across a small table, the things his eyes are on while she's talking. So they belong to **his half of the duet**: she catches him looking, names it flatly, carries on. **She never inventories herself.** That's the difference between this and a strip-club lyric.
- **Hips are how she leaves.** They're the only item on that list that's inherently in motion — a neck sits there, hips walk across a room and turn away at the end of a song. Timing is what she runs on, so hips get the structural jobs: arriving, sitting down slowly, and the last thing anyone sees.
- **Scent is the near-yes's own sense, and it's hers to offer.** Everything in the looking palette is taken by his eyes; smell is the one thing she gives, and it only exists at one distance — which does the geography of the near-yes for free. It carries no anatomy, so it can run hotter than anything else in the song while staying entirely printable (*"close enough to smell my promises"*). One scent per song, in the near-yes, never twice: what she's wearing, what she isn't wearing, gin, cigarettes, rain on a coat, the kitchen, the room.
- **Low, warm, unhurried, faintly amused.** She's the only person at the table who isn't hungry. The delivery is never breathy, never a purr, never trying — trying is fatal. She sounds like someone who has all night and knows she doesn't need it.
- **Dry wit, one or two beats per song.** A joke at the mark's expense that's affectionate rather than sharp, and one at her own. Peggy Lee's flatness, not a wink.
- **Short lines, big pauses.** The pause does more than the line. Write for a singer who is comfortable letting four bars go by.
- **Repetition that changes meaning.** A line from verse one returns in the final hook meaning something warmer, or something final. Shared with the lurker spec, used in the opposite direction.
- **One line that lands.** As in all four siblings — one line per song that snaps into plain sense amid everything oblique. **The construction *"___ where ___ should ___"* is banned** — it was the family nod across the earlier specs and it is used up. New shape every song.

**The heat dial.** The lineage runs from *velvet* to *ice*: Julie London singing to one person in a small room at 2am, through Peggy Lee's amused flatness, out to full Jessica-Rabbit cinematic noir with the big brass and the doorway silhouette. Pick a point per song — **velvet-intimate ← → cinematic-noir** — and match it to the mark. Someone who wants to be forgiven goes velvet. Someone who wants to be dangerous goes noir.

## Song structure (the template)

Standing-bass torch-song form, so treat this as a skeleton hung on a slow 4/4 that never hurries. Crucial principle: **the band is on her side and knows the ending.** It leans in when she does, it drops out when she wants the quiet, and it starts up again the moment she stands — because there's another table.

1. **Verse 1** — brushed drums, upright bass, Rhodes or piano, muted trumpet down a corridor. Voice close, low, warm, forward in the mix. Opens **mid-conversation**, answering something unsaid. She establishes the table, the evening and how good it's going. Faintly amused.
2. **Pre-hook** — the trumpet steps nearer, the bass walks. Small lift, not a build. One line where the temperature goes up a notch and she notices where he's looking.
3. **Hook** — **the invitation imperative** as opener: two to four words that sound exactly like a yes (*"sit closer"*, *"stay a while"*, *"order something else"*). The family's imperative slot at its warmest — nobody's barking it, she's offering it. Carries **the one line that lands**, and ends on **the closing line** — the last line of the hook, fresh every song, sung completely flat. It's about the person at the table, never about her record.
4. **Verse 2** — **the mark's specific hunger.** One dated, concrete, small thing about what he wants to be told: dangerous, forgiven, interesting, young, chosen, the exception. Named and particular. Then **the looking** — what his eyes have been doing, in his half of the duet, caught and named kindly.
5. **The near-yes** — the signature move. **Everything drops to brushes and one muted trumpet.** She goes closest, warmest, quietest — the whole song appears to arrive. He's certain. *The listener is certain.* Then she stands up. No slap, no laugh, no explanation. One short line on the way out, and the band comes back in underneath her before he's worked out what happened.
6. **Final hook** — same words, and they mean something else now because of what just didn't happen. **One word changes** and that's the ending. Ends on the **band playing on** — another table, another evening, the trumpet not remotely troubled — never a resolving chord, never a landing.

The **near-yes is the signature move** — the direct equivalent of gypsy-emo's half-time-to-double-time bridge, institutional hardcore's mock-calm-monologue-that-detonates, dissoc's voice-eaten-by-the-machine dissolve and lurker's drop-out. It's the moment the arrangement and the subject become the same thing: the closest anyone has ever got, and it's still nothing. **Don't skip it, don't let her explain herself in it, and don't let her be unkind.**

## The recurring devices (checklist)

- **The invitation imperative as hook opener** — two to four warm words that sound like a yes and promise nothing (*"sit closer"*, *"stay a while"*, *"keep talking"*). The family's imperative slot at its warmest. (Rhymes with gypsy-emo's *play it quick*, hardcore's *sit down, shut up*, dissoc's *part it out*, lurker's *be nice about it*.)
- **The near-yes** — the drop to brushes, the arrival that doesn't, the standing up. Never cruel, never explained.
- **The closing line** — the last line of the hook, new every song, delivered flat. The strongest ones are aimed at him, not at her record: *"there's no right answer to it, and that's not an accident"*, *"I'm exactly what the room thinks"*, *"say it and I'll let you keep it"*. Never a tally, never a slogan.
- **The silent duet** — she answers what wasn't said; you hear his half in the gaps. At least one **quote-back** per song.
- **The caught look** — his eyes named without complaint, from the surfaces palette, in his half.
- **The mark's specific hunger** — one named thing he wants to be told about himself, concrete and slightly embarrassing. This is the per-song variable.
- **The one line that lands** — one per song, plain sense amid the deniable. Never the banned *where/should* construction.
- **The scent line** — one per song, in the near-yes, the only sense she offers rather than he takes. Fresh every time.
- **Hips as the exit** — the last thing seen, every time, in some form.
- **Dry wit** — one beat at his expense, affectionate; one at her own.
- **Refused pronoun** — second person only, no gendered noun anywhere.
- **Repetition that changes meaning** — a verse-one line returning altered.
- **The band knows the ending** — it leans in with her, drops out for the near-yes, and starts again the moment she's up.
- **Warm, unresolved tonality.** Where gypsy-emo says harmonic minor, dissoc says blown-out-and-clipping and lurker says dusty-and-unresolved, the note here is **major-with-a-flat-somewhere** — smoky sevenths and ninths, a raised sixth, jazz voicings that promise a resolution and take the long way round. The chords behave exactly like she does.

Save the size for nothing much. **This style has no climax** — that's the joke. It gets warmer, closer and quieter, and then it gets up and leaves.

## Handling the subject matter

The brief is **suggestive, never explicit**, and the craft advice and the safety advice are the same advice, which is the sign it's the right rule.

- **No acts, in any tense, ever.** Not described, not implied as having happened, not planned in detail. The catalogue contains no events. There's a table, a conversation, an enormous amount of certainty, and a woman standing up.
- **The surfaces palette is a hard ceiling** — ass, hips, legs, thighs, calves, feet, bosom, neck, mouth, fingers. Nothing beyond it, ever. These are things visible across a table, and they live in **his looking**, not her self-description.
- **Everyone is an adult and obviously so.** Bar, late, a check on the table, a career's worth of evenings behind her. Never a schoolgirl register, never anything young, never "little". Not negotiable and not interesting to test.
- **She has all the power and no malice.** The mark is never humiliated, never mocked to his face, never punished. He leaves happy and slightly confused. If a draft has her enjoying his suffering, it's the wrong song.
- **No harm anywhere in the frame.** She's a succubus in the folkloric sense — she feeds on the wanting — but nobody is damaged, nothing is taken from anyone against their will, and no one is worse off. The comedy and the tragedy are both that they'd all do it again tomorrow.
- **Deniability is the mechanism, not a dodge.** If a line only works when it's explicit, the line is weak, not brave. Rewrite it until the listener has to supply the second meaning themselves — that's when it actually gets hot.

## Suno prompt notes

**Notation rules — these are hard, and getting them wrong audibly wrecks the take.** Same rules as the lurker spec; they're generic to Suno.

- **`[Square brackets], on their own line` = direction.** Not sung. Every performance instruction goes here — dynamics, arrangement, who's singing, what the band does, how a section ends. Be as verbose as you like; it costs nothing.
- **`(Parentheses)` = backing vocal, and the contents get SUNG.** So parentheses hold **only words you want to hear**. Never a description, never a stage direction, never a label. `(low, amused) stay a while` sings the words "low, amused".
- **Never use em-dash-wrapped prose as a direction.** `— (she stands up) —` is not a tag and can be read aloud. Convert every one into a bracketed tag on its own line: `[She stands up. The band comes back in underneath her.]`
- **Nothing between the brackets and the lyrics.** Every line is either a `[tag]` on its own line or words to be sung. There is no third category.

Test before you paste: **read only the un-bracketed text and ask whether you'd be happy hearing all of it.**

- Paste the **bracketed section tags** (`[Verse 1 — brushed drums, upright bass, muted trumpet down a corridor, voice close and low]`, `[The near-yes — everything drops to brushes and one muted trumpet]`, etc.). The near-yes depends entirely on the arrangement actually thinning out, and the tags are the only way to get that.
- **State "solo female vocal, no backing vocals, no gang vocals" explicitly in the style prompt** and again in the final tags. Suno stacks a crowd behind any repeated hook by reflex, and a crowd destroys this style specifically — the whole premise is one person at one table.
- Ask for the vocal **close, low, warm and unhurried, slightly behind the beat.** Explicitly ask it *not* to belt. The failure mode is a big brassy show-tune delivery, which turns her into a performer and loses the intimacy.
- The **near-yes is the least predictable part** — regenerate several times to find a take where the band genuinely strips back and the singer goes *quieter* rather than bigger. A take that swells there kills it. (Same job as chasing the crack in gypsy-emo's shout-back, the *turn* in hardcore's monologue, the swallow in dissoc's dissolve, the silence in lurker's drop-out.)
- Style prompt for the **velvet late-night core** (recommended default):
  ```
  slow smoky torch jazz, brushed drums, upright bass walking, Rhodes and jazz piano, muted trumpet distant, warm room reverb, major seventh and ninth voicings, unhurried, low warm amused solo female vocal close-miked and behind the beat, intimate, no backing vocals, no gang vocals, no big finish
  ```
- **Cinematic noir variant** (full Jessica Rabbit — for marks who want to be dangerous, and for the colder songs):
  ```
  cinematic film noir jazz, sultry big band, muted brass swells, lush strings, vibraphone, upright bass, slow burlesque swing, dramatic and glamorous, low sultry solo female vocal, 1940s nightclub, no backing vocals, unresolved ending
  ```
- **Sparse piano-and-voice variant** (the warmest and most exposed — for marks who want to be forgiven):
  ```
  sparse late night jazz ballad, solo piano and upright bass only, brushes barely there, room tone, no strings, intimate cracked warm female voice recorded very close, rubato, unresolved, solo vocal only, no percussion in places
  ```
Core instrument/texture palette: brushed kit, walking upright bass, jazz piano or Rhodes, muted trumpet, vibraphone, tremolo guitar, clarinet, muted trombone, optional lush strings for the noir end. **No solos except the trumpet, and the trumpet never finishes its phrase.** The **muted trumpet is the solo** — the way the violin carries gypsy-emo, the spoken monologue *is* the solo in institutional hardcore, the sampler in dissoc and the loop in lurker trip-hop.

## Reference example (a finished song in this style)

**"Ask Me Again"** — Lucy Might. The mark wants to be dangerous. Sits mid-dial, tipped velvet. The invitation imperative is *"sit closer"*; the hunger is that he wants to be told he's trouble; the near-yes is three inches and a coat.

```
[Verse 1 — brushed drums, upright bass, jazz piano, muted trumpet down a corridor. Voice close, low, warm, slightly behind the beat. She is answering something that was never said out loud.]
No, go on. I like the way you tell it.
You've been building to that line since half past nine.
And you're right, I did notice.
I notice everything. It's the job.

You keep saying you're bad news.
Bad news. You said it twice.
Nobody's ever had to tell me that about themselves —
but I'll take your word.

[Pre-hook — trumpet steps nearer, bass walks. Small lift. Not a build.]
Your eyes have been at my neck since the second drink
and you think I mind.

[Hook — barely bigger. Low, warm, flat, unhurried.]
sit closer
stay a while
I have never once said no
and I have never once had to
ask me again
ask me again

[Verse 2 — same tempo, vibraphone added, nothing else changes.]
Nineteen years I've done this room.
I could tell you what you'll order before you do.
You want the version of you that I'm looking at —
that's the whole tab, that's the whole evening.

Your hands went quiet when I sat down.
Your eyes did the rest — the mouth, the hips, back up.
And I let you. I always let you.
That part's free.

[Hook — identical words. Do not change them. The verse changed them for you.]
sit closer
stay a while
I have never once said no
and I have never once had to
ask me again
ask me again

[The near-yes — everything drops. Brushes and one muted trumpet only, very quiet. She goes closer, warmer, softer. It appears to arrive.]
Closer.
Closer than that.
Close enough to smell my promises.
That's the closest anybody's got.

[She picks up her coat and stands. The band comes back in underneath her before he has worked out what happened.]

[Final hook — one word changed. Low, amused, entirely warm, walking away.]
sit closer
stay a while
I have never once said no
and I have never once had to
ask me again
ask me tomorrow

[Outro — the trumpet takes a phrase and doesn't finish it. Band plays on for another table. No resolving chord, no ending.]
```

Companion pieces written to the same spec:

- **"Save Room"** — the mark wants to be forgiven. Pulled to the sparse piano-and-voice variant, the warmest and most exposed thing in the catalogue. Invitation imperative: *"tell me the rest"*. The hunger is absolution and she is genuinely kind about it, which is worse. The near-yes is her hand on the back of a chair. The closing line is the softest in the catalogue and lands hardest.
- **"The Long Way Round"** — the mark wants to be told they're the exception. Pulled hard to cinematic noir, big brass, strings, a doorway. Invitation imperative: *"order something else"*. The dated specific is a hotel bar and a year. The near-yes happens in a lift. Nobody in the song is unhappy and nothing at all occurs.

## Don't let it calcify (rotate the surfaces, keep the bones)

The institutional-hardcore spec had to grow a rule banning its own reflex, the dissoc spec had to ban three, the lurker spec had to ban the family's shared nod outright. This style's trap is different and worse: **an undefeated narrator has no arc, so every song is the same song.** The fix is to rotate everything about the evening and nothing about her. Across a batch, these **must** change song to song:

- **The mark's hunger.** The per-song variable and the most important one. Dangerous, forgiven, interesting, young again, chosen, the exception, listened to for an hour, the one who finally. Never the same want twice.
- **The invitation imperative.** Fresh every time. Never reuse *"sit closer"*.
- **The closing line.** New every song, and never a variation on a previous one. If it could be printed on a T-shirt, it's wrong.
- **The one line that lands.** New shape each song: an admission, a number, a fact stated once, something she has never said to anyone said as if it were nothing.
- **The near-yes.** New geography every time — how close, and what she picks up on the way out. Never the coat twice.
- **The scent.** One per song and never repeated. *"Smell my promises"* belongs to "Ask Me Again" and stays there.
- **The looking.** Rotate which surfaces, and rotate what his hands do while his eyes are busy.
- **The arrangement and the heat dial.** Velvet, noir or piano-and-voice, matched to the hunger.

What *should* recur — these are the style, not tics, and cutting them breaks it: the warmth without cruelty, the refused pronoun, the silent duet, the deniable line, the near-yes, the trumpet that never finishes, and the band playing on for the next table. **Rotate the surfaces; keep the bones.** If two consecutive songs share a shape, the second one's wrong — rewrite it.

## When writing a new one

Pick **one mark** and **one hunger** — the specific thing they want to be told about themselves. Pick your point on the **heat dial** (velvet-intimate ↔ cinematic-noir) and your **arrangement** to match it: forgiveness goes sparse and warm, danger goes noir. Find the **invitation imperative**, the **closing line** and the **geography of the near-yes**, and build outward from those three; they're the load-bearing ones. Then cut every line that says the quiet part, and put a pause where it was.

Keep the whole arc: mid-conversation and already going well up front, the hook that offers everything and promises nothing, the hunger named in verse two, the near-yes that gets closer than anyone has ever got, and the band starting up again for the next table. Above all keep it **warm, unhurried and undefeated** — she means every word, she is never cruel, nothing at all happens, nobody leaves angry, and the song ends by the trumpet not finishing its phrase rather than by anything arriving. If a draft gives you a sneer, a crowd, a victim or an event, you've written something else — take it back to the table, and let her stand up.
