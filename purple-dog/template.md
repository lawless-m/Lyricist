# Institutional Hardcore — Song Style Spec
 
A reusable brief for writing songs in the "institutional hardcore" style — the early-Suicidal-Tendencies / Venice-'83 lineage. Hand this to Claude with an instruction like *"another song in the style of this document"* and it should produce a new, standalone lyric set that shares the voice, structure and signature moves below — not a rewrite of the example.
 
Sibling spec to **gypsy-emo**. Same house, opposite temperature: where gypsy-emo turns the wound inward and mournful, this one turns it outward and furious. The devices deliberately rhyme across the two.
 
---
 
## The core idea
 
The engine of this style is not rage. It's **the reasonable maniac**: a sane person calmly explaining his side, being driven to fury *because nobody believes him*. The anger is the symptom; the disbelief is the wound.
 
The defining move — the "Institutionalized" trick — is that the meltdown is triggered by something absurdly **small**. A denied Pepsi. A guy at a desk telling you to lower your voice. The pettiness *is* the point: tiny denials stack up until a person who asked for almost nothing is treated like a threat, and the world's verdict ("he's crazy") becomes self-fulfilling the moment he finally shouts. So the lyric always works two layers at once — the trivial surface grievance, and the whole life of dismissal underneath it.
 
The rule that keeps it honest (the equivalent of gypsy-emo's "instrumentation leads the emotion"): **the trigger must be genuinely petty and the narrator must be genuinely reasonable at the start.** If he's already ranting in verse one, it's just a hardcore song. The whole thing lives or dies on the *distance travelled* — polite-and-quiet to detonation. Earn the scream.
 
The other half of the trick is the crowd. Emo is alone-in-your-bedroom lonely. This is **lonely turned communal** — but instead of a caravan aching in unison, it's a room full of people who all got written off the same way, snapping at once. So every song is built around a shout-back line where the crowd doesn't comfort the narrator — it **agrees with him against the world**, or names the truth he's still denying about himself.
 
There's usually a class wound under the personal complaint, worn casually. The lost file, the letter that says you don't qualify, the help that was promised and wasn't there. Keep it as texture, not a lecture — it's why he's tired, not a policy position.
 
---
 
## Song structure (the template)
 
1. **Verse 1** — spoken or half-spoken, tense but *reasonable*. Tight, quiet drums; guitar barely there. He's talking TO you, explaining, keeping it together. Conversational. First person.
2. **Pre-chorus** — guitars start chugging, drums tighten. The reasonableness visibly cracks — one line where the calm slips.
3. **Chorus** — fast, shouted, full band, gang backing vocals. Opens with a **stacked imperative** — what they keep telling him to do (*"sit down, shut up, take your pills, act glad"*). Contains the accusatory parallel (fresh grammatical shape per song — see the checklist) and a direct accusation. Ends on the **contradicting gang shout-back** — the crowd shouting the truth he won't (*"I'm fine, I'm fine —" / (gang) "HE'S NOT FINE"*).
4. **Verse 2** — spoken again, more manic this time. **The specific mundane trigger** — the small, stupid, real thing that set him off. Named, concrete, almost embarrassing in its smallness.
5. **Breakdown** — the signature move. A **spoken, mock-calm monologue** that starts *very* reasonable ("okay, I'll be calm, watch — this is me being calm") and escalates line by line into a scream. This is the moment the two halves of the style audibly fuse: the sane man and the maniac in the same breath. Hand off on the snap from spoken to screamed.
6. **Final chorus** — the biggest. Full gang vocals. The shout-back **flips**: the crowd stops naming him and starts *claiming it* — *"HE'S NOT FINE"* becomes *"WE'RE NOT FINE."* Lonely-together, hardcore version.
The **mock-calm-monologue-that-detonates is the signature move** — the direct equivalent of gypsy-emo's half-time-to-double-time bridge. It's where the style earns its name. Don't skip it, and don't let it start already-angry.

## The recurring devices (checklist)
 
A new song should hit most of these:
 
- **Spoken-reasonable verse** — conversational, talking directly to the listener, defensive but composed. The calm is the setup for the drop.
- **The petty trigger doing enormous work** — one small, concrete, almost-funny denial that stands in for a lifetime of dismissal (*not* the Pepsi — find a fresh one: the coffee, the headphones, the guy who told him to lower his voice before he'd even raised it). Put it in verse 2, named and specific.
- **The "am I crazy or are they?" flip — shown, never stated.** The narrator's grip should *look* like it's holding while it visibly isn't. Do NOT let him announce his own state — **the construction *"I'm not crazy / I'm not paranoid / I'm not calm — I'm just the only one still ___"* is banned.** It reads as the narrator reading you his own diagnosis off a card, and it's become the reflex this spec keeps defaulting to. Instead, dramatise the fraying through **action and object**: the notebook by the phone that's nearly full, the hand that won't stop, the fact he's quietly stopped telling his daughter. Let the *contradicting gang shout-back* carry the "or are they?" — he protests, the crowd overrules him — so the flip lives in the structure, not in a line where he labels himself. If a draft names the label, cut the line and show the behaviour that would earn it.
- **Stacked-imperative chorus opener** — the list of instructions the world keeps barking at him: *"sit down, shut up, take your pills, act glad."* Two-to-four short commands.
- **The accusatory parallel** (the emotional-thesis slot, shared with gypsy-emo, bent accusatory) — a two-clause mismatch couplet aimed at the institution, in a **fresh grammatical shape every song**. The original *"I've got ___ where ___ should ___"* construction is retired catalogue-wide (see trope library), and each replacement is spent after one use (the *"you trust the log, not the room"* two-beat from no-show is already gone). The slot is mandatory; no filling recurs.
- **A direct accusation** to authority / "you" near the end of the chorus — second person, pointed. *"You heard it coming — you always hear it coming."*
- **The list** — somewhere, a reeled-off inventory of the ways he's been labelled or let down (*"I did quiet when they moved us on, I did quiet when they lost the file…"*). Repetition that escalates.
- **The contradicting gang shout-back** — the crowd shouts the truth he's denying (*"HE'S NOT FINE"*).
- **The communal flip** in the final chorus — the shout-back turns from *he* to *we*. The crowd claims the anger with him.
- **Minor key, stop-start dynamics, fast.** Quiet-verse / eruption-chorus contrast is everything.
Keep verses composed and conversational; save the size and the speed for the choruses and the detonating breakdown. And keep it **defiant, not despairing** — this style is about reclaiming your voice against people who wrote you off, anger pointed outward. It's "I'm done being quiet," not "I'm done."

## Reference example (a finished song in this style)
 
**"I'm Fine"** — the man who's told to calm down before he's even raised his voice.
 
```
[Verse 1 — spoken, flat, reasonable, tight quiet drums]
No, I'm not mad. Did I say I was mad?
I'm just saying it's the fourth time this week
that somebody looked at me like I'm the problem in the room.
I keep my head down. I do the thing. I don't ask for much.
I asked for one thing —
 
[Pre-chorus — guitars chug in, building]
and you looked at me like I asked you for the world,
like wanting to be left alone's a crime
 
[Chorus — fast, shouted, full band, gang backing]
so sit down, shut up, take your pills, act glad
I lowered it, I lowered it — and they wrote me down as mad
I've got walls where a door should be, I've got you telling me it's me
I'm fine, I'm fine, I'M FINE —
(gang: HE'S NOT FINE)
 
[Verse 2 — spoken, more manic]
You wanna know what set me off? You really wanna know?
It wasn't the rent. It wasn't the job. It wasn't the letter that said I don't qualify.
It was a guy. At a desk. Who told me to lower my voice.
I wasn't even loud yet. I hadn't started.
But he heard it coming — he always hears it coming.
 
[Pre-chorus — chug, building]
and now it's on my record, now it's who I am,
one raised voice and you've got me all figured out
 
[Chorus — fast, shouted, gang backing]
so sit down, shut up, take your pills, act glad
I lowered it, I lowered it — and they wrote me down as mad
I've got walls where a door should be, I've got you telling me it's me
I'm fine, I'm fine, I'M FINE —
(gang: HE'S NOT FINE)
 
[Breakdown — spoken, mock-calm, detonating into a scream]
Okay. Okay okay okay. I'll be calm. Watch. This is me being calm.
This is the voice you wanted, right? Nice and quiet. See? I can do quiet.
I've done quiet my whole life.
I did quiet when they moved us on. I did quiet when they lost the file.
I did quiet when they said there's help — and then there wasn't —
and everybody clapped 'cause I was so — well —
so ADJUSTED —
so — LET IT — OUT—
 
[Final Chorus — biggest, full gang vocals]
sit down, shut up, take your pills, act glad
I lowered it, I lowered it, and it never made them glad
I've got walls where a door should be —
and the quiet never saved me, so I'm done being quiet
I'm fine, I'm fine — (gang: HE'S NOT FINE)
— (ragged, all together): WE'RE NOT FINE
```
 
**A note on the reference example:** *"I've got walls where a door should be"* is the retired
*where/should* construction; *"sit down, shut up, take your pills, act glad"* is this song's
imperative and stays here; and the final-chorus landing *"and the quiet never saved me, so I'm
done being quiet"* was one of three founding recordings across the house sharing the now-retired
*"and the [X] didn't/never [save/fix] me…"* closing formula (see trope library). The slots stay;
those fillings are spent.

Companion piece written to the same spec: **"Lower Your Voice"** (the desk, the label, the file — told from the crowd's side, everyone in the room who got the same verdict), tempo pushed to the crossover-thrash variant, shout-back flipped from the first line so the *we* is there the whole way through.

## When writing a new one
 
Pick a fresh **petty trigger** (never reuse the Pepsi, the coffee or the desk guy) and a fresh **label** the world has stuck on him — that's what keeps each song a sibling, not a copy. Keep the whole arc: reasonable-and-quiet up front, the mock-calm monologue that snaps, the gang shout-back, and the *he → we* communal flip at the end. Change the trigger and the stacked-imperative opener to fit the mood. Vary whether the arrangement is raw-'83, crossover-thrash or melodic-skate. Keep it defiant and outward-pointed — the wound is being *disbelieved*, and the ending is reclaiming the voice, not surrendering it. And show the fraying, never state it: no *"I'm not crazy / I'm not paranoid — I'm just the only one still ___"* — earn that read through the notebook, the shaking hand, the going-quiet, and let the crowd's shout-back be the only place his sanity is ever named.
