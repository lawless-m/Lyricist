# Dissociative Hardcore — Song Style Spec
 
A reusable brief for writing songs in the "dissociative hardcore" style — the Lip Critic / digital-hardcore / electropunk lineage: two drummers, samplers instead of guitars, a preacher at the mic, and the whole thing running too hot. Hand this to Claude with an instruction like *"another song in the style of this document"* and it should produce a new, standalone lyric set that shares the voice, structure and signature moves below — not a rewrite of the example.
 
Third sibling spec to **gypsy-emo** and **institutional-hardcore**. Same house, third temperature. Gypsy-emo turns the wound inward and mournful; institutional hardcore turns it outward and furious; this one turns it **sideways and numb**. The devices deliberately rhyme across all three — but the biggest departure is in the *words*: the other two tell a story, this one lets the story come apart on purpose. Read the collage-method section before you write a line.

**Where the band name comes from.** We wanted robots to fold the laundry while we made music and wrote poetry — we got robots that make music and write poetry while we fold the laundry. **Laundry** is that inversion, worn as a name. It's the same joke "The Machine is Crying" is already built on (the kettle writes the villanelle, he minds the lint, "I'm not even sad about it"), and it's the actual gravity well under the whole band, not just one song's — worth keeping live as a possibility, not spending it all at once.

A newer, sharper way of saying the same thing: **"the machine gets the emotions while I go through the motions."** Used as the legible spike in `second-draft` — now logged in the trope library and retired like any other device.

## The core idea
 
The engine of this style is not grief and not rage. It's **overload metabolised as numbness**: a person reporting something genuinely unbearable in the wrong register — flat, distracted, faintly amused — because the feed already flattened it before it reached him. The horror is real; the *flatness* is the wound. Where institutional hardcore is the reasonable maniac driven to fury because nobody believes him, this is the reasonable man who has stopped being able to feel the thing he's describing, and is dimly aware that's worse.
 
The defining move — the equivalent of institutional hardcore's "denied Pepsi" trigger — is **the atrocity and the ad sharing one thumb-swipe**. A body and a snack, a genocide and a giveaway, your own identity being spent in Ohio, all delivered by the same glowing rectangle at the same brightness. The style always works two layers at once: the grotesque surface image, and underneath it the quiet fact that the narrator can no longer tell which images are supposed to hurt.
 
The real fusion this style dramatises is **human + machine collapsing into each other**. The human half is the sweat: two live drummers playing *against* each other, a frontman with cult-leader-in-a-stadium-announcer's-booth energy, a body in a room. The machine half is the churn: samplers where the guitars should be, blown-out digital texture, everything overheated and unstable. The thesis is that these two don't sit side by side — the machine *eats* the human, and the song is the sound of that happening in real time.
 
The rule that keeps it honest (the equivalent of gypsy-emo's "instrumentation leads the emotion" and institutional hardcore's "the trigger must be petty"): **the affect must be under-scaled to the horror, and there is no catharsis on offer.** Three hard constraints:
 
- **Under-react.** If the narrator is screaming *about* the thing, it's just a hardcore song. If he's mourning it, it's emo. He has to be weirdly *fine* with the unbearable — that wrongness is the whole payload.
- **Withhold the release.** Gypsy-emo earns its catharsis (the doubled bridge); institutional hardcore earns its scream (the detonation). This style **refuses** the catharsis on purpose, because resolving would be a lie about how the feed actually feels. Earn the numbness. Do not fake a release you haven't got.
- **Let the meaning come apart.** The lyric itself has to dissolve — not into nonsense, into *collage* (see the next section). Language decohering on the page **is the numbness enacted**. It's "instrumentation leads the emotion" applied to the words: the sense drains out the way feeling has. A tidy narrative would be lying about the state the song is in.
The other half of the trick is the crowd. Emo is alone-in-your-bedroom lonely. Institutional hardcore is lonely-turned-communal-and-defiant — a room agreeing against the world. This is **lonely-together-and-numb**: a room chanting the same short phrase over and over until the words stop meaning anything, everyone dissociating in unison. The shout-back doesn't comfort (emo) or accuse (hardcore) — it's a **mantra worn down to noise**, the sound of a crowd going blank together.
 
There's usually an identity wound under it, worn casually: theft of self, the copy who's living your life better than you, the paperwork that can't confirm you're you. Keep it as texture, not a thesis — it's why he's thin, not a point about surveillance.

## Writing the words — the collage method
 
This is the part that separates the style from its siblings and the part that's easy to get wrong in both directions. The lyrics **read** like random words. They are not random — "write random words" is a useless brief and won't sound like anything. They're **controlled free-association around a fixed grotesque palette, chosen for sound, anchored by one repeated mantra, all orbiting a theme nobody ever states.** Get the method right and it reads unmoored-but-inevitable; skip it and you get either boring word-salad or an emo song wearing a costume.
 
The rules:
 
- **Concrete and physical, never abstract.** Marrow, teeth, drains, conveyor belts, packing peanuts, the good kidney. Grotesque bodily and retail nouns — not feelings. The images are vivid; it's the *connections between them* that are missing, not the images.
- **A floating "you"/"I" that never resolves.** Could be him, could be you, could be the addressee or the copy — deliberately never pinned. That unfixed pronoun is most of why it feels adrift.
- **Sound picks the word as often as sense.** Keep a near-malapropism if it *sounds* right ("barely" landing as "barley"). Phonetics lead, meaning trails. Rhyme and internal echo drive the line further than logic does.
- **Non-sequitur and self-cancelling lines as texture.** A statement and its own contradiction, left uncancelled: "exactly what you came for / but there's no one there to see." Surreal intrusions welcome — a goblin at the returns desk, a tooth in the reeds — the way their titles do it ("My Wife and the Goblin," "In the Wawa (Convinced I Am God)").
- **One unstated theme as a gravity well.** Nothing names the subject, but every image is pulled toward the same place — theft, bodies, disappearance, consumption, the self sold for parts. It's a gravity well, not a plot. If a reader can *feel* the centre without you stating it, it's working.
- **A mantra anchor.** One short phrase that keeps returning and accumulating ("everything at arm's length"). This is the single thing standing between collage and actual noise — it's the spine the drift hangs off, and it's what the crowd catches.
- **One flash of legibility.** Amid the smear, let exactly one line snap into plain sense and land hard — it hits *because* everything around it is dissolved. This is where the family "___ where ___ should ___" parallel goes (bent dissociative), used as a spike of clarity, then abandoned before it resolves into a thesis.
**The coherence dial.** Their own catalogue runs a spectrum: "Milky Max" is a fairly followable paranoid line delivered like a cult leader; "Talon" is smeared to the edge of sense. So pick a point on the dial per song — **followable-paranoid ← → pure-collage** — rather than committing to full salad every time. Mid-dial (a gravity well you can feel, images you can't quite connect, one legible spike) is the sweet spot and the safest place to write from. Push toward pure-collage only when the arrangement can carry it.
 
**How to actually generate it:** start from the gravity well and a palette of eight or ten grotesque concrete nouns. Free-associate a stream of short physical images off them — overproduce, don't edit yet. Then prune hard *for sound and for grotesque-logic*, not for narrative sense; keep the lines that ring, cut the ones that explain. Drop in the mantra at the anchor points, plant your one legible spike, and leave the joins missing. The gaps are the composition.

## Song structure (the template)
 
Lip Critic songs are collage more than verse-chorus-bridge, so treat this as a skeleton to hang the moves on. Crucial principle: **keep the structure rigid while the flesh dissolves.** Suno still needs the bracketed dynamics and a listener still needs the mantra to grab onto, so the *sections* stay legible even when the *words* go to collage.
 
1. **Verse 1** — fast, clipped, dry. Sampler stabs, both kits chattering. First person, flat. He reports a wrong situation as an image-stream, *without the correct amount of alarm*. Under-reacting; hollowed out.
2. **Pre-hook** — the sampler grabs the last word of the verse and starts chopping it; the drums fold in on themselves. Textural build, not emotional — it thickens, it doesn't lift.
3. **Hook** — fast, chanted, gang backing. Opens with a **consumption imperative** — the instruction the feed keeps barking (*"part it out, price the pieces…"*). Carries the **one flash of legibility** (the parallel line), a **question with no addressee** aimed at his own body, and ends on the **mantra** — a short phrase stacked toward meaninglessness.
4. **Verse 2** — clipped again. The image-stream thickens and the non-sequiturs get stranger. The two layers show at once: grotesque surface, unstated centre.
5. **The dissolve** — the signature move. **The voice eaten by the machine.** He starts talking (mock-composed, thinning), and the sampler chops / pitches / loops the vocal until the human is indistinguishable from the digital churn. No eruption. No scream. It just gets denser and then *cuts*.
6. **Final hook** — the biggest, whole room, but the mantra now arrives **degraded** — pitched, smeared, half-swallowed. Communal numbness at full volume. Ends not on release but on an abrupt cut or a **loop left running** and faded — never a landing.
The **voice-eaten-by-the-machine dissolve is the signature move** — the direct equivalent of gypsy-emo's half-time-to-double-time bridge and institutional hardcore's mock-calm-monologue-that-detonates. It's the moment the human and the machine audibly become one thing. Don't skip it, and **don't let it resolve into catharsis** — the machine wins this one.

## The recurring devices (checklist)
 
Word-level craft lives in the collage-method section above; these are the structural/hook devices a new song should still hit:
 
- **Under-reacting verse** — reports the unbearable in a flat, distracted, faintly amused register, as an image-stream. The wrongness of the calm is the setup, and no drop is coming to relieve it.
- **Consumption-imperative hook opener** — the list of instructions the feed barks: *"part it out, price the pieces, move the meat."* Two-to-four short commands, all about consuming / scrolling / spending / scrapping. (Rhymes with gypsy-emo's band-instruction opener and hardcore's stacked "sit down, shut up.")
- **The flash of legibility** — one line, and *only* one, snaps into plain sense amid the smear and lands hard *because* everything around it is dissolved. This is a **slot, not a fixed phrase** — fill it differently every song. The family "___ where ___ should ___" parallel is *one* way to fill it (the nod to the sibling specs), but it's the exact construction this style will default to until it becomes a tic, so use it sparingly and rotate it out most of the time. Other fillings for the same slot: a flat declarative that's too calm to be sane (*"I checked the tracking twice"*), a plain admission (*"I don't want it back"*), a single concrete fact stated once and dropped (*"the dog has my grandmother's name"*). If the last song used where/should, this one doesn't.
- **A question with no addressee** near the end of the hook — not to a lover (emo) or to authority (hardcore), but to nobody, about his own reality. Vary the shape: *"is that my hand?"* is one, but don't let *"is that my ___?"* become the reflex — sometimes it's a statement he can't finish, sometimes a number he reads out, sometimes he just stops.
- **The mantra shout-back** — a short phrase chanted, stacked and repeated until it goes to noise. The crowd's whole job is to dissolve the words with him. **Invent a fresh one each song** — do not reuse "everything, everything, everything ___"; that's Lip Critic's own signature tic, and copying its shape turns your songs into karaoke. Change the phrase, the number of repeats and the rhythm every time.
- **The dissolve** — spoken vocal chopped / pitched / looped into the sampler; human absorbed by machine; no release.
- **The degraded final chorus** — the mantra returns pitched and smeared, and the track cuts or loops out rather than ending.
- **Identity / theft as the usual gravity well** — the copy spending your money, the self you're relieved to be rid of, the bank asking if you're still you. Worn casually.
- **Overheated, unstable, digital production; no guitars as the backbone; stop-start collage.** The sampler is the lead instrument. Texture over key — where the other specs say "minor / harmonic minor," here the tonality note is *blown-out and clipping*.
Save the size for the hook and the dissolve — but the size here is **density, not catharsis**. It gets heavier, not higher.

## Reference example (a finished song in this style)
 
**"Parted Out (Convinced I'm the Package)"** — identity theft rendered as collage: the self scrapped, priced and shipped for parts, narrated in the flat voice of a delivery notification. Sits mid-dial — a gravity well you can feel (the body sold off / worn by someone else), images you can't quite connect, one legible spike.
 
```
[Verse 1 — fast, clipped, flat, sampler stabs]
Woke up a little lighter, they took the left hand in the night
left a barcode on the wrist and a note that said it's fine
somebody's got my teeth now, somebody's got the good kidney
I hope they steam it, I hope they plate it, I hope they scan me and they read me
peanuts in the ribcage where the wanting used to sit
two-day on the shoulder — they insured it, they can't miss
and I'm not even mad. I checked the tracking twice.
 
[Pre-hook — sampler grabs a word, chops]
read-me / read-me / (read) / (me)
— and the drums buckle in —
 
[Hook — fast, chanted, gang backing]
so part it out, part it out, price the pieces, move the meat
I've got a receipt where a reason should be —
(gang) part it out, part it out
everything I was is in a box in someone's grip
and would you look at that — is that my hand in the window?
is that my hand? on sale?
 
[Verse 2 — clipped, image-stream, non-sequitur]
The goblin at the returns desk won't take me — wrong receipt,
says I'm final sale, says I'm as-is, says the skin's got too much wear.
I said define wear. It restocked me anyway.
Milk on the conveyor. A tooth in the reeds.
Someone left five stars: "arrived fast, would be him again."
Barley a face left now. Barley a —
 
[The dissolve — spoken, thinning, then chopped and looped into the sampler]
I can be whole. Watch. I've still got the — I've got the — hold on —
I've got the / got the / (the) / (got—)
— (vocal pitches down, loops, the sampler eats the singer) —
in stock— in stock— out of— in st— (—ock)
— no scream. it just packs tighter. part it out. then cut. —
 
[Final Hook — biggest, whole room, pitched and degraded]
part it out, part it out, price the pieces, move the meat
and he can keep the beat now, keep the teeth, keep me
(gang, smeared) part it out, part it out
somebody's wearing my whole life and it fits him like a grip
(chanted, dissolving): part it out / part it / part— / (—)
— (loop left running, faded, no ending) —
```
 
Companion piece written to the same spec: **"Self-Checkout (Convinced I'm the Cashier)"** — the coherence dial shoved to the far end, near pure-collage: the gravity well (theft of self) present only as a smell, the images barely joining, the mantra reduced to a single repeated word, tempo pulled to the slower *insidious churn*. The dissolve buries the vocal so deep the human barely surfaces at the end.

## Don't let it calcify (rotate the surfaces, keep the bones)
 
The sibling institutional-hardcore spec had to grow a rule banning its own reflex (the *"I'm not paranoid — I'm just the only one still ___"* self-diagnosis) because the style kept defaulting to it until every song shared one spine. This style has the same trap and more surfaces to fall into, because the collage method makes it tempting to reach for the same few load-bearing phrases every time. So across a batch, these must **change song to song** — none is a template to refill:
 
- **The mantra.** Never twice the same, and never the *"everything, everything, everything ___"* shape — that's the band's own tic, not yours. Fresh phrase, fresh repeat-count, fresh rhythm each time.
- **The flash-of-legibility line.** The *"___ where ___ should ___"* parallel is an occasional family nod, not the every-song spike. If the last song used it, this one finds another way to land its one clear line.
- **The question with no addressee.** Don't let *"is that my ___?"* become the reflex. Sometimes a statement he can't finish; sometimes a number; sometimes he just stops.
- **The imperative opener.** Vary the verb-count and the shape — not always *"verb it, verb it, X the Y."*
- **The grotesque palette.** A new gravity well and a new set of concrete nouns every song (shipping/parting-out is spent now).
What *should* recur — these are the style, not tics, and cutting them breaks it: the numb under-reacting register, the collage method itself, the voice-eaten-by-the-machine dissolve, the withheld catharsis, and the no-landing ending. **Rotate the surfaces; keep the bones.** If two consecutive songs share a phrase-shape, the second one's wrong — rewrite it.
 
---
 
## When writing a new one
 
Start from a fresh **gravity well** and a fresh **grotesque palette** (never reuse Talon's drain-body, or this one's shipping/parting-out imagery). Pick your point on the **coherence dial** (followable-paranoid ↔ pure-collage) and your **arrangement** (fast-core, slow-churn, club-adjacent) — those two dials are what keep each song a sibling, not a copy. Then run the collage method: free-associate off the palette, prune for sound and grotesque-logic not narrative, plant one mantra anchor and one legible spike, leave the joins missing.
 
Keep the whole arc: flat-and-under-reacting up front, the consumption-imperative hook, the mantra chanted to noise, and the voice-eaten-by-the-machine dissolve that **refuses to resolve**. Above all keep it **numb, not furious and not mourning** — the wound is that he can't feel it, the meaning drains out of the words the way feeling drained out of him, the machine wins the dissolve, and the song ends by cutting out or looping, never by landing. If a draft gives you a clean story or a clean catharsis, you've written the wrong genre — smear it, and bury the ending.
