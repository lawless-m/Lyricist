# Lurker Trip-Hop — Song Style Spec
 
A reusable brief for writing songs in the "lurker trip-hop" style — the Portishead / Beth Gibbons lineage: a dusty slowed breakbeat, a Rhodes, vinyl crackle, and one thin close-miked female voice that sounds like it's being recorded against its will. All songs written to this spec are by **Guessed**. Hand this to Claude with the instruction **"song for Guessed"** — optionally with a theme, a dial position or an arrangement — and it should produce a new, standalone lyric set that shares the voice, structure and signature moves below, not a rewrite of the example. With no further steer, pick an unused theme, an unused arrangement, and go.
 
Fourth sibling spec to **gypsy-emo**, **institutional-hardcore** and **dissociative-hardcore**. Same house, fourth temperature. Gypsy-emo turns the wound inward and mournful; institutional hardcore turns it outward and furious; dissociative hardcore turns it sideways and numb. This one turns it **inward and silent** — the wound is never reported at all. The devices deliberately rhyme across all four, but the crowd device inverts, and that inversion is the whole style. Read the "no crowd" section before you write a line.
 
**The title word is load-bearing and multivalent — keep all its meanings live.** A lurker is someone who has read every thread for fifteen years and posted in none of them: fully present, entirely unregistered, holding an opinion nobody will ever receive. It's a net-native word and a kind one — lurkers aren't despised, they're simply not counted, which is the exact temperature of this style. It also arrives out of the same 4chan lineage as the four words (*lurk moar* being the instruction that made her one). And underneath the affectionate sense sits the older one: something that lurks is something waiting, unresolved, not yet dealt with. She is both at once — the quiet reader, and the thing in the room that hasn't been addressed.
 
The narrator is therefore always **a reader, not a speaker**. She knows everything about the room she's in. The room knows nothing about her. She isn't withholding out of strategy any more; it's just what she does now.
 
**The name is part of the brief.** *Guessed* is a past-tense verb with no subject and no object — she was guessed at. Guessed wrong, guessed male, guessed harmless. Rooms decided what she was and never checked, and she let them, and that's the arrangement the whole catalogue is about. Said aloud it's *guest*: present, no account, no standing, nothing kept after the tab closes. Written down it's a word people will get slightly wrong forever, which is on-theme enough to be a feature. If a lyric could be sung by someone who has been correctly identified, it isn't a Guessed song.

**Billing name vs. lyric content.** The artist is credited externally (Suno, streaming) as **Emily Guest** — but that name is metadata, not material. It must never appear inside a lyric: the moment she's correctly named on the page, the song stops being a Guessed song, per the rule above. **Wrong** names are fair game and are in fact the whole point — a name she made up, a name someone's had wrong for years, a name on a cup or a cred that isn't hers — because the correct name is the one thing every song withholds. *"Emma"* is a good candidate: someone once called her *"Em"* (natural enough, close to home), and somebody else — not wanting to presume the familiarity of a nickname he hadn't earned — politely lengthened it to *"Emma"*. Not a mishearing at all, really: a small act of good manners that got the actual name more wrong than carelessness would have. Exactly the register this style wants — no villain, just a decent impulse landing sideways.

**Visual identity.** Thumbnail/cover art uses a trained LoRA to keep her likeness consistent across the catalogue — business attire, situations that vary, always smiling. The constant, composed smile in the artwork is the exact same thesis as the songs, just rendered visually: everything's fine on the surface, and the surface is the whole point. Keep the visual brief and the lyric brief in agreement — competent, composed, giving nothing away.

## The core idea
 
The engine of this style is **the double bind**: something happened to her, and reporting it is the one thing that would disqualify her from being believed. So she doesn't report it. The song is what's left over.
 
That shape is the same every time, whatever the subject:
 
- Say you're a woman in the room and the room decides that's all you were ever doing.
- Object to being written off and you've proved you wanted special treatment.
- Say you regret the choice and you've betrayed everyone who fought for you to have it.
- Say what the man on the street did and you've become the thing you spent twenty years despising.
- Say you're lonely and you've admitted you're a loser, which is the accusation you were managing in the first place.
Every one of those is a door that locks from her side. **The song is never about the event. It's about the silence after it** — the recalibration, the tiny administrative decision to just not mention it, made so quickly and so often that it's become a personality.
 
The defining move — the equivalent of institutional hardcore's denied Pepsi, gypsy-emo's named grave, dissociative hardcore's atrocity-next-to-an-ad — is **the disproportionately small response to the large thing**. She typed "ha". She went and made a cup of tea. She muted herself before the round started. She closed the tab. The gap between what happened and what she did about it *is* the song. Never dramatise the event; dramatise the shrug, and let the size of the wound be inferred from how carefully she isn't touching it.
 
The rule that keeps it honest (the equivalent of gypsy-emo's "instrumentation leads the emotion", hardcore's "the trigger must be petty", dissoc's "under-react"): **flat delivery, dry wit, and absolutely no self-pity.** This style has one fatal failure mode and it's whinge. Portishead is never self-pitying — it's too cold and too well-dressed for that. The narrator is smart, funny in a bleak way, entirely aware of how she sounds, and would rather die than ask you to feel sorry for her. The moment a line begs, the song is dead. **She is not asking to be rescued. She is filing a report nobody requested.**
 
And the second constraint: **she is not right about everything, and the song must not try to make her right.** She's a person with a ledger that doesn't balance, not a position paper. Where a lyric could be a thesis or a symptom, make it a symptom.

## No crowd — the inverted device
 
The other three specs all end in a room full of people. Gypsy-emo is lonely-*together*: a caravan aching in unison, a shout-back to be caught by a crowd. Institutional hardcore is lonely-turned-communal-and-defiant: a room that all got the same verdict, agreeing against the world. Dissociative hardcore is lonely-together-and-numb: a crowd chanting a mantra to noise, dissociating in unison.
 
**This style has no crowd, and that absence is the point.** There are no gang vocals. There is no shout-back. There is nobody else in the room and there never will be — that's the subject matter. Putting a crowd in a lurker trip-hop song is the single worst mistake available, because the entire premise is a person whose experience is structurally unshareable.
 
So the family's communal device is **replaced by her own voice, doubled**. She is the only one who answers her. Use it deliberately:
 
- **The double** — the same line layered a half-beat behind itself, slightly flat, slightly ahead, so it sounds like a room and isn't one.
- **The answer that's also her** — a call-and-response where both parts are hers, one dry and forward, one drowned in reverb at the back of the hall.
- **The wordless vocal** — a hum, an *ah*, a melody with no words where a chorus would be. Portishead does this constantly and it does the emotional work a crowd would do elsewhere.
Mark it explicitly in the section tags (`[Final hook — her own voice doubled and layered. no other voices. ever.]`) so nobody, human or Suno, gets tempted to fill the room.

## Writing the words — sparse, oblique, repeated
 
This style's craft section, the way collage-method is dissociative hardcore's. The words are the opposite problem to the dissoc spec: not too smeared, too *few*.
 
- **A whole song is maybe 120 words.** Portishead lyrics are tiny. Most of the track is space, loop and voice. If a draft is a page of text, it's the wrong genre. Cut it in half, then take out the explaining.
- **Short flat lines, plain vocabulary.** No inversions, no poeticism, no reaching. "I know when not to speak" beats anything ornamental. The register is a clever woman being deliberately unclever because she's tired.
- **Repetition is the engine, and repetition changes meaning.** The same line three or four times isn't padding — it lands differently each pass because of what's happened around it. Build for that: a line in verse one that returns in the final hook meaning something worse.
- **Concrete and domestic, never grand.** The tea, the tab, the headset, the loading screen, the letter, the bus stop, a specific year. Small nouns doing large work. Dates and numbers are especially good — *two thousand and four*, *fifteen years*, *four words* — they're flat, factual, and utterly damning.
- **Refuse the noun.** Never name the thing directly. Count it, describe its shape, mention its length, say what she did after. **The four words are never quoted.** Naming it lets the listener file it; withholding it makes them lean in and makes her seem like someone who's had to not-say it for years, which she is.
- **Dry wit, one or two beats per song.** A joke she makes at her own expense, delivered completely flat. This is what keeps it out of whinge and it's the most Beth-Gibbons-adjacent thing there is — the wry line that isn't funny by the second listen.
- **Second person aimed at nobody in particular.** "You" who could be a man, a friend, a movement, herself at twenty-two, the listener. Never resolve it. (Rhymes with dissoc's floating pronoun, but here it's *withholding*, not drift.)
- **One flash of legibility.** As in the sibling specs — one line that snaps into plain sense and lands hard because everything around it is oblique. Usually the drop-out line. Only one per song.
**The temperature dial.** Their catalogue runs warm-to-cold: *Dummy* is torchy, dusty, almost romantic; *Third* is frozen, mechanical, unforgiving, no reverb, Krautrock pulse. Pick a point per song — **dusty-torch ← → frozen-mechanical** — and match the subject to it. Regret goes warm. Humiliation goes cold.

## Song structure (the template)
 
Trip-hop is loop-first, so treat this as a skeleton hung on a repeating four bars. Crucial principle: **the loop is indifferent, and it never stops for her.** It doesn't build with her, respond to her, or resolve for her. It was going before she started and it's going after she finishes.
 
1. **Verse 1** — dry slowed breakbeat, Rhodes, crackle. Voice close, thin, forward in the mix. First person, flat, present tense. She establishes the arrangement she's made with the world and how well it works. Faintly proud of how good she is at it.
2. **Hook** — barely bigger. Strings or theremin smear in, the low end thickens. Opens with a **quoted instruction** — something she was told so long ago she now says it to herself in her own voice (*"be nice about it"*). Carries **the one line that lands** and ends on **the question she already knows the answer to**, repeated twice.
3. **Verse 2** — the specific incident. One dated, concrete, small event. Named year. What was said, by shape not by content. Then **what she did instead of responding** — the disproportionate shrug.
4. **Hook** — same words, worse now. Do not change them. The verse changed them for you.
5. **The drop-out** — the signature move. **Everything cuts.** No beat, no bass, no reverb. One line, dry and alone, in plain sense — the one true thing she'd never say out loud. Then the loop returns *unchanged*, as if it didn't hear, because it didn't.
6. **Final hook** — her own voice doubled and layered, no other voices ever. One word of the hook changes and it's the whole ending. Ends on the **loop running alone** for a few bars and fading, or the crackle continuing after the music stops — never a chord, never a landing, never a resolution.
The **drop-out is the signature move** — the direct equivalent of gypsy-emo's half-time-to-double-time bridge, institutional hardcore's mock-calm-monologue-that-detonates and dissociative hardcore's voice-eaten-by-the-machine dissolve. It's the moment the production and the subject become the same thing: she finally says it, in the clear, to an empty room, and the machine carries on regardless. **Don't skip it, don't let more than one line into it, and don't let the loop acknowledge it.**

## The recurring devices (checklist)
 
- **The quoted instruction as hook opener** — two to four words she was told and has since internalised, now in her own mouth (*"be nice about it"*, *"don't make it a thing"*, *"you knew what it was"*). The family's imperative slot, at its lowest temperature: nobody's barking it at her any more, she's doing it herself, which is worse. (Rhymes with gypsy-emo's *play it quick*, hardcore's *sit down, shut up*, dissoc's *part it out*.)
- **The disproportionate shrug** — the small administrative response to the large thing. The tea, the *ha*, the closed tab, the mute. Put it at the end of verse 2, undramatised.
- **Refusing the noun** — count the words, don't quote them; describe the shape, don't name the act.
- **The dated specific** — a year, a number, a count. Flat and factual against everything oblique.
- **The unspent expertise** — one line, dropped without emphasis, showing she knows the room better than anyone in it: she can name every person in the thread, she's read the whole thing twice, she knew the answer, she was better than him. Never stated as a complaint. It's the lurker's particular ache — total attendance, zero standing — and it does more damage delivered as a shrug than as a grievance. **The construction *"nobody['s] [ever/never] asked"* is banned** — it recurred across enough songs (unflagged until now) to be the second nod this style keeps defaulting to. Show the zero-standing ache through a concrete detail instead of stating that nobody asked.
- **The one line that lands** — one line per song, third in the hook, that says the thing plainly amid everything oblique. **The construction *"___ where ___ should ___"* is banned** — it was the nod shared across all four sibling specs and it is now used up; if a draft reaches for it, cut the line and find another shape. Fill the slot fresh every song: a plain admission (*"I answer to a word I made up"*), a fact stated once and dropped, a number read out flat, a thing she has never told anyone said as though it were nothing. Never twice the same shape.
- **The question she already knows the answer to** — near the end of the hook, repeated twice, unanswered. Not to a lover (emo), not to authority (hardcore), not to nobody about his own body (dissoc) — this one is aimed at **her own past self**, and the answer is available and unbearable. *"So who was I keeping quiet for?"*
- **The drop-out** — beat cuts, one naked line, loop returns indifferent.
- **The doubled voice instead of a crowd** — never gang vocals. See the "no crowd" section.
- **The wordless vocal** — a hum or *ah* where a chorus would be, at least once.
- **Repetition that changes meaning** — a line returns later meaning something worse.
- **Dry wit, no self-pity, flat delivery.**
- **Loop-first production; the arrangement never resolves.** Where gypsy-emo says minor/harmonic minor and dissoc says blown-out-and-clipping, the tonality note here is **dusty, narrow and unresolved** — minor keys, unresolved sevenths, everything slightly out of tune, and the drums *behind* the beat.
Save the size for nothing. **This style has no big moment.** It gets closer, colder and quieter. The peak is the drop-out, and the drop-out is silence.

## Handling the subject matter
 
These songs live in territory the sibling specs don't, and the craft advice and the ethics happen to be the same advice. The brief is **genuine representation of people who don't get a voice** — so the failure mode isn't offence, it's the two ways of getting it wrong: turning her into a poster, or turning her into a victim. Both are lies about a real person.
 
**Territory this style covers.** Social anxiety and the specific loserhood of a woman who isn't the woman she was supposed to be. Regretting career over children. Being a woman who plays games and is never quite in the room. The four words, and the fifteen years of pre-emptive male-presenting that followed. Feeling let down by a movement that told her what she'd want. Supporting something in principle and being hurt by it in person, then finding she isn't allowed to say so. A friend's win she can't feel happy about, performed anyway (`be-happy-for-her`). One theme per song, or two that share a shape — never all of them at once.

**Banked, unused territory** — double-bind situations spotted but not yet written; check this list before reaching for a theme, so a fresh one doesn't get proposed twice:
- Grief that's aged out of its welcome — old enough that people have started saying "still?"
- Catching herself becoming the mother she swore she wouldn't, and "you're just like her" landing as a compliment from someone else.
- A partner's small, old lie she chose to let stand years ago, and still lets stand.
- Being told she's "aged so gracefully" — a compliment built entirely on the thing she's not supposed to mind about.
(Already spent, for reference: remembering everyone's birthdays and never being remembered back — that's `when-was-it-decided`, not a fresh well.)
 
**The rules:**
 
- **First person, one woman, one life. Never a group, never a statistic, never "they."** Not "men do this" — *he* did this, on this street, in this year. Not "immigration has consequences" — *a hand on her arm outside the Co-op at half nine and the two weeks of deciding not to mention it.* Demographic claims turn a song into a leaflet and a leaflet is both worse art and worse faith. The particular is the only honest unit and it's also the only one that moves anyone.
- **The song is the silence, not the incident.** For every one of these themes, the thing she can't do is *report it*. Write the double bind, not the grievance. This is why the whole spec holds together: the four words and the street and the movement and the childlessness are all the same locked door, and the song is her standing on the wrong side of it being reasonable about it.
- **Let her be wrong, and don't correct her.** She may conclude that nature always wins and the whole project was a lie sold to her. That's a real thing real women think, arrived at through a real life, and the song should let her think it without either endorsing it or arguing with it. Write it as **a ledger, not a manifesto** — the accounting of one person's specific losses, from which she has drawn a conclusion. A listener who disagrees with her conclusion should still believe her losses. If a line reads like it wants to win an argument, cut it; if it reads like something bitter she arrived at alone at 2am, keep it.
- **No villains, and no absolution either.** The men in these songs are barely present — a shape at a desk, four words, a hand. They're weather. Making them monsters lets the listener off; the point is that nothing dramatic enough to complain about ever happened, which is exactly why she couldn't.
- **She's complicit and she knows it.** She muted herself. She typed *ha*. She agreed. She was *pleased to be let in*. The self-implication is what makes it a real voice rather than a grievance, and it's the most painful material available.
- **Never a solution, never a lesson, never a moral.** Nobody learns anything. The loop runs out.

## Reference example (a finished song in this style)
 
**"Handle"** — Guessed. fifteen years of passing as no-one online, and the four words in 2004 that started it. Sits mid-dial, tipped cold. The gravity well is the arrangement she made with a room, and how well it worked.
 
```
[Verse 1 — dry slowed breakbeat, Rhodes, vinyl crackle, voice close and thin]
Fifteen years I've been a boy in here
and I'm good at it.
I know the words. I know the lag.
I know when not to speak.
 
There's a name I made in ninety-nine —
it doesn't look like me.
It fits a little better every year.
I keep it clean.
 
[Hook — strings smear in, low end thickens, still small]
be nice about it
be one of the boys about it
I answer to a word I made up
and nobody's ever asked
so who was I keeping quiet for
so who was I keeping quiet for
 
[Verse 2 — same loop, one string line added, nothing else changes]
I said "she" once. About myself.
Two thousand and four. A thread about a game.
Somebody typed four words
and the room agreed with him.
 
And I laughed. I typed "ha".
I typed "ha", and I went and made a cup of tea,
and I never said it again.
Fifteen years. Never said it again.
 
[Hook — identical words, no bigger]
be nice about it
be one of the boys about it
I answer to a word I made up
and nobody's ever asked
so who was I keeping quiet for
so who was I keeping quiet for
 
[Wordless — hum over the loop, four bars, no words]
 
[The drop-out — everything cuts. no beat, no bass, no reverb. one line, dry, alone]
I was twenty-two, and I was so pleased to be let in.
 
[The loop returns unchanged, indifferent]
 
[Final hook — her own voice doubled and layered, the second voice half a beat behind and drowned at the back of the hall. no other voices. no gang vocals. ever.]
be nice about it
be one of the boys about it
I answer to a word I made up
and I never wanted them to ask
(I never wanted them to ask)
so who was I keeping quiet for
(who was I keeping quiet for)
 
[Outro — loop runs four bars alone, vinyl crackle continues after the music stops, no ending]
```
 
Companion pieces written to the same spec:
 
- **"Thirty-Nine"** — the career/children ledger, pulled to the sparse acoustic variant, warm and much more exposed. Quoted instruction: *"you've got time"*. The dated specific is a consultant's appointment. The drop-out line names a child who doesn't exist, once, and never again. The wit beat is her being good at her job.
- **"Half Nine"** — the street outside the Co-op, pulled hard to the frozen *Third* variant, no reverb, motorik. One man, one hand, one evening, never described as anything but a shape and a distance. The entire song is the fortnight afterwards and the sentence she drafts and deletes. Quoted instruction: *"don't make it a thing"*. The drop-out line is about what she'd have to become to say it out loud, and the loop doesn't care about that either.

## Don't let it calcify (rotate the surfaces, keep the bones)
 
The institutional-hardcore spec had to grow a rule banning its own reflex, and the dissoc spec had to ban three. This style's trap is that its restraint makes every song look the same on the page — so across a batch, these **must** change song to song:
 
- **The quoted instruction.** Fresh every time. Never reuse *"be nice about it"*.
- **The drop-out line.** The one legible spike. New shape each song — sometimes an admission, sometimes a date, sometimes a name, sometimes a sentence she can't finish. It must never be a conclusion.
- **The one line that lands.** New shape every song — admission, fact, number, silence broken. Never the banned *"___ where ___ should ___"*.
- **The disproportionate shrug.** New small action every time — never the tea twice.
- **The unspent-expertise line.** New shape every song. Never the banned *"nobody['s]
  [ever/never] asked"*.
- **The theme.** One per song. Don't write the same wound in a new coat.
What *should* recur — these are the style, not tics: the flat unpitying register, the tiny word-count, the indifferent loop, the doubled voice standing in for a crowd that will never arrive, the drop-out, and the non-ending. **Rotate the surfaces; keep the bones.**
 
---
 
## When writing a new one
 
Pick **one** theme and one specific woman. Pick your point on the **temperature dial** (dusty-torch ↔ frozen-mechanical) and your **arrangement** (Dummy-dusty, Third-frozen, sparse-acoustic) to match it — regret warm, humiliation cold. Find the **quoted instruction** she says to herself, the **dated specific**, and the **disproportionate shrug**, and build outward from those three; they're the load-bearing ones. Then cut the lyric until it's about 120 words and every remaining line is short, plain and slightly withheld.
 
Keep the whole arc: composed-and-competent up front, the hook that doesn't lift, the incident undramatised, the drop-out that says the true thing to an empty room, and the loop that carries on regardless. Above all keep it **quiet, dry and unpitying** — the wound is that it can't be reported, the double bind is the subject and not the backdrop, she's complicit and knows it, nobody arrives, nothing is resolved, and the song ends by the loop running out rather than by anything being said. If a draft gives you a crowd, a catharsis, a villain or a moral, you've written one of the other three — strip it back, and let the crackle have the last word.
