[Intro — cold, spoken, mechanized pulse]
My father used to say the graves are hungry, back when three came due in the same week and the clay wouldn't give an inch faster than a man could swing a pick. He didn't mean it as a metaphor. Neither did I, the year it turned out to be true.

[Verse 1 — clipped, technical, controlled]
Six feet standard, four for a child — the depth's never up for debate,
clay runs two foot solid here before it breaks, so the pick comes out before the spade.
Third generation on this ground, I learned the pace before I learned the pain,
know which plots drain slow by August, which ones flood first when it rains.

[The wink — spoken, flat, cold]
I dug hers myself. Four foot, not six.
I didn't let anyone else near the spade that day.

[Hook — chanted, stacked]
Feed it a name, it don't ask whose. Feed it a hundred, it don't say when.
The ground was hungry before I was born, and it'll be hungry again.
I dig the same six feet for a stranger as I dug four foot for my own,
and I'd pick the spade back up tomorrow, and I'd dig it just as deep.

[Verse 2 — rapped, escalation]
Ledger's been in my own hand since I could lift the spade,
her name's third from the bottom — some mornings I open to that page before anything else.
The ground doesn't stop needing plots dug to let a digger grieve on schedule,
so I buried the Hendersons that same week. Same pace. Same hands.

[Final hook — chanted, weightier, full band]
Feed it a name, it don't ask whose. Feed it a hundred, it don't say when.
THE GROUND WAS HUNGRY BEFORE I WAS BORN, AND IT TOOK WHAT IT WANTED THEN TOO.
I dig the same six feet for a stranger as I dug four foot for my own,
and I'll pick the spade back up tomorrow, and I'll dig it just as deep.
