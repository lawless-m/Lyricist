[Intro — cold, spoken, mechanized pulse]
Rückenfigur. German for a figure with its back to you. Friedrich painted them so you'd stand at their shoulder and never see what they saw. I've done it my whole career. I didn't plan it that way.

[Verse 1 — clipped, technical, controlled]
Three-quarter turn, weight on the back foot, collar up if there's wind in the scene,
never paint the ear, never paint the jaw — the second you do, they stop being anyone.
I've sold sixty-odd of these to people who never ask why the face is always missing,
they just stand in front of it and go quiet, which is the whole point, the only point.

[The wink — spoken, flat, cold]
She modelled for three hundred and sixty-two of them. I never painted her face.
She never asked me to, and I never asked her to turn around.

[Verse 2 — rapped, escalation]
She's gone now, and I went through the studio looking for one where she'd turned even slightly,
caught mid-glance, caught checking the light — anything. There isn't one.
Three hundred and sixty-two canvases of the same collarbone, the same set of shoulders, the same weight on the back foot,
and I taught myself the trick so well I never broke it, not even by accident.

[Verse 3 — rapped, quieter, hushed]
I still turn every model three-quarters now, still say collar up, still say weight on the back foot,
and some evenings, in the right light, from behind, at the right distance, I let myself believe it's her,
for exactly as long as it takes them to turn around and ask if the pose is right,
and then it's just an hourly rate and a stranger's shoulders again.

[Final hook — chanted, weightier, full band]
I paint the back so you can stand where she stood — that's the whole of the technique, nothing more to it.
THREE HUNDRED AND SIXTY-TWO CANVASES, AND NOT ONE OF THEM HER FACE.
It was about you, every time, standing at your own shoulder, looking where I pointed.
I'd paint the same back again tomorrow, and I still couldn't tell you the colour of her eyes.
