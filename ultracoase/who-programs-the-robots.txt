[Intro — cold, spoken, mechanized pulse]
Everyone wants to know who programs the robots. Nobody asks who programmed the man who wrote the code. I can answer both, if you've got the time. Neither answer is short.

[Hook — chanted, stacked]
Ask who programs the robots and the room points at a company, a committee, a policy —
I don't point anywhere. It was me, alone, at a desk, one number off the top of my head.
That number's running in machines I've never even seen the floor of.
I'd write the same rule again tomorrow, and I'd still be guessing when I did.

[Verse 1 — clipped, technical, controlled]
Every safety rule starts as a number somebody has to pick,
too close, too fast, too heavy — turn the feeling into a threshold, write it down, ship it.
Committee signs off on the concept. Nobody signs off on the number.
Numbers don't come from committees. Numbers come from someone's gut, alone, at a desk.

[Verse 2 — rapped, escalation]
That threshold's in fourteen machines now, three sites, two countries, one line of code,
copy-pasted forward every time somebody needed the same shape of problem solved.
Nobody who copied it ever called to ask where the number came from,
and I never called to tell them, because until this year, I never had to.

[The wink — spoken, flat, cold]
Forty milliseconds. That's how long the arm waited before it caught her hand. The report called it a sensor fault. It wasn't the sensor. It was my number.
I'd shared a break room with her every week for six years and never told her which number I'd picked for her hand.

[Final hook — chanted, weightier, full band]
Ask who programs the robots and the room points at a company, a committee, a policy —
FORTY MILLISECONDS. MY NUMBER. NOT THE SENSOR'S FAULT.
That number's running in machines I've never even seen the floor of.
I'd write the same rule again tomorrow, and I'd still be guessing when I did.
